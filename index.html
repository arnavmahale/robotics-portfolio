Project Overview

Problem, goals, and core idea

The goal of this project was to design and control a robot arm that can play a simplified version of beer pong: repeatedly tossing a ping pong ball from a fixed start position into one or more cups placed on a table at a known distance.

Our team focused on modeling the throwing motion of the end-effector, mapping desired launch velocities to joint trajectories, and iteratively calibrating the throws to improve accuracy. I particularly focused on connecting the kinematics and tuning process to the physical behavior we observed in the lab.

  • Modeled the arm and end-effector pose for a throwing motion.
  • Implemented forward/inverse kinematics to generate joint targets.
  • Designed a parameterized throwing trajectory (angle, speed, timing).
  • Empirically tuned parameters to maximize shot consistency.

Perception & Cup Detection

Locating the target cup using vision

To ensure consistent throws, the robot must know where the cup is on the table. I used a camera view of the workspace and performed cup detection, drawing a bounding box around the target and estimating its 3D position (X, Y, Z in meters) relative to the camera frame.

Technical Details

Kinematics, trajectory design, and tuning
Kinematics & Poses

We used the arm’s kinematic model to compute joint angles that achieve a desired release pose for the end-effector (position and orientation) relative to the table. This ensured the ball started from the correct location and direction before being released.

  • Defined a pre-throw pose and a release pose in 3D space.
  • Solved inverse kinematics to get joint configurations for these poses.
  • Ensured joint limits and feasible motion between the two states.

The throw is modeled as a fast motion between these poses, with timing chosen to create the right end-effector velocity at release.

Throw Parameterization & Tuning

Instead of analytically solving the full ballistics problem with perfect dynamics, we used a parameterized throw and tuned it experimentally.

  • Launch angle and direction of the end-effector.
  • Speed of the motion (duration of the throw segment).
  • Exact release timing / joint velocity profile.

By logging the hit/miss outcomes for different parameter sets, we iteratively improved consistency and accuracy toward a reliable shot.

Key observations
  • Small changes in release timing or angle had a large effect on where the ball landed.
  • Once tuned, the robot was much more repeatable than a human throw, but sensitive to small position changes of the cup or table.
  • Modeling physics is helpful, but real-world testing is essential to capture friction, compliance, and arm dynamics.

My Role & Takeaways

What I contributed and what I learned

This was a team project. I contributed to the overall system design, helped implement the kinematics and control logic, and worked on tuning the throwing motion and designing the experiments used to evaluate performance.

  • Gained hands-on intuition for how kinematics and motion planning connect to physical behavior.
  • Experienced the gap between ideal models and real hardware dynamics, especially at high speeds.
  • Practiced designing controlled experiments and using data to improve performance.

If we had more time, I would extend this work with automatic parameter search (e.g., optimization or reinforcement learning) and add perception so the robot can detect cup positions instead of relying on a fixed setup.
